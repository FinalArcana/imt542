## The Poor(ish) Example: Thai Meteorological Department (TMD)
The Thai Meteorological Department (TMD) publishes a wide range of weather information including forecasts, rainfall, storm tracks, radar imagery, and climate summaries. Even with this broad coverage, the overall information structure still lands on the low end of portability as described in OECD Section 3.2. TMD has introduced a numerical weather prediction API, but it only covers forecast data. Most of the agency’s datasets are still delivered through static CSV files, shapefiles, PNG images, HTML tables, or PDF bulletins. This fits the OECD description of low portability as a one time download of unformatted data with no built in interoperability.

The range of TMD’s data is large, but the scope of machine readable access is narrow. Forecasts are the only dataset category with API support, and even that API is split across multiple endpoints without a unified schema or bulk retrieval options. Important datasets such as historical rainfall, storm tracks, radar archives, and climate summaries are not available through any API. Users must manually download ZIP archives or scrape HTML tables, which makes automation difficult and limits integration with disaster management systems, agricultural tools, or hydrological models.

Update frequency is also uneven. Forecasts refresh regularly, but rainfall archives, storm tracks, and climate summaries update monthly or annually. Radar imagery is only available as static PNG files, which cannot be queried or combined with other datasets in a structured way. Chapter 5 emphasizes that portability depends on dynamic, standardized, user centered data flows. TMD’s mixed formats and scattered access points do not meet that standard.

Interoperability is the biggest challenge. TMD does not provide machine readable metadata, consistent variable names, or links between datasets. There is no unified API gateway, no OpenAPI or Swagger documentation, and no real time streaming or push mechanisms. Developers must manually interpret formats and build custom parsers, which mirrors the same issues seen in other low portability examples such as the Seattle Pet Licenses dataset.

TMD could improve portability by consolidating all datasets under a single RESTful API, offering JSON or GeoJSON outputs, publishing machine readable metadata, and providing real time or near real time endpoints for rainfall, radar, and warnings. Connecting datasets across agencies such as the Royal Irrigation Department or the Department of Disaster Prevention and Mitigation would also strengthen both vertical and horizontal interoperability. These steps would move TMD closer to the OECD’s high portability model of broad, real time data sharing through a common API.

## The Good Example: OpenWeatherMap API
Even with just the free tier, OpenWeatherMap shows what a high portability information structure looks like. It fits well with the OECD’s description of the upper end of portability, where data is shared in real time across digital services through a common API. The free tier provides standardized, machine readable access to current weather, basic forecasts, and geocoding through a unified RESTful API. Although the free tier has limits on historical depth and model detail, the underlying structure still supports strong interoperability.

The free student tier adds even more value by including access to limited historical weather data. This gives students and academic users the ability to work with past conditions for research, modeling, and analysis without needing a paid subscription. Having historical data available, even in a restricted form, expands the scope of what users can build and supports more advanced applications such as climate studies and time series analysis.

The range of data in the free and student tiers includes current conditions, 3 hour forecasts, basic air quality data, global geocoding, and selected historical records. While narrower than the paid tiers, this is still broader than what many national meteorological agencies offer for free. The scope is global, allowing developers to query any coordinate on Earth. This stands in contrast to TMD’s Thailand only datasets and fragmented access structure.

Update frequency in the free tier is consistent and predictable. Current weather data updates every 10 minutes to 1 hour depending on the location, and forecasts refresh several times per day. Even though the free tier does not provide real time streaming, the regular update cycle supports most consumer and enterprise use cases.

Interoperability is one of OpenWeatherMap’s strongest qualities. All endpoints use standardized JSON schemas, consistent naming conventions, and predictable URL structures. Documentation is clear, multilingual, and includes code examples. The free tier also integrates smoothly with GIS tools through coordinate based queries, which supports horizontal interoperability across mapping, transportation, and IoT platforms. This is a sharp contrast to TMD’s reliance on CSVs, shapefiles, and static images that require manual preprocessing.

The free tier does have limits such as rate caps and restricted historical depth, but these limits affect volume rather than portability. The system remains API first, standardized, and globally accessible.

OpenWeatherMap could improve portability by expanding free access to additional historical data, offering lightweight GeoJSON layers, or providing more detailed metadata about model sources. Even with these limitations, the free and student tiers still demonstrate the OECD’s high portability criteria through their unified API architecture, standardized formats, and predictable update cycles.
