## Availability

The project uses publicly accessible USDA Foundation Foods and Food at Home Monthly Area Prices (F MAP) datasets, which are available through open JSON and CSV endpoints without authentication. All identifiers such as FDC IDs and EFPG codes are preserved to maintain traceability and alignment with FAIR principles. No sensitive or proprietary data is used, and all transformations are documented to support reproducibility and portability consistent with OECD 2021 guidance.

## Limitations

The USDA datasets contain gaps and inconsistencies, and the mapping between foods and EFPG categories relies on heuristic matching that may introduce classification errors. A key limitation is that the Food at Home Monthly Area Prices dataset only extends through 2018, so cost metrics do not reflect recent inflation or market changes. Price values represent historical averages and may not capture regional or brand level variation. The analysis focuses on calories and protein per dollar, which simplifies nutrition and may not generalize beyond U.S. food systems.

## Ethics

### Virtue Ethics

The project emphasizes transparency, accurate attribution, and responsible communication of data. It aims to model virtues such as honesty and humility by clearly documenting data sources and avoiding overstated claims about nutritional or economic insights.

### Consequential Ethics

The analysis can support understanding of food affordability, but simplified metrics could be misinterpreted as dietary guidance or used to justify inequitable food policies. To reduce potential harm, results are framed as descriptive rather than prescriptive, and users are cautioned against drawing medical or policy conclusions from the outputs.

### Non‑consequential Ethics

The project avoids personal data, maintains fidelity to USDA sources, and upholds duties related to data integrity and provenance. It adheres to established norms for responsible data handling and does not alter USDA data beyond necessary cleaning steps.

## Societal Impact

The project may help users explore relationships between nutrition and food cost, supporting research and public understanding. However, simplified cost efficiency metrics risk reinforcing reductive views of nutrition or obscuring structural inequities in food access. Misuse could lead to narrow interpretations of affordability or overlook cultural and community food practices. Clear documentation and transparent methods aim to reduce these risks and encourage responsible interpretation.
